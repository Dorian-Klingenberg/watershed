# World Systems

## Ancient Infrastructure
The ancient civilization built:
- massive aqueducts
- water transport systems
- mountain ice-dome melt mechanisms
- weather-affecting hydrological controls
- region-spanning civil works that outlasted their creators

Current societies know some structures by function or folklore, but they do not possess full design knowledge.

## Present-Day Dynamics
Infrastructure failures create symptoms such as:
- broken aqueducts causing marsh growth or water loss
- overactive channels causing floods or unstable wetlands
- deserts appearing where water routing changed
- transportation corridors opening or closing unpredictably
- settlements adapting with folk engineering and imperfect local rules

## Environmental Transformation

Terrain states are not static.

Environmental conditions emerge from simulation values such as:

water saturation
soil permeability
vegetation growth
infrastructure leakage

Example transitions:

dry farmland -> irrigated farmland
irrigated farmland -> oversaturated wetland
wetland -> swamp
swamp -> peat marsh

## Modern Adaptations to Ancient Infrastructure

Ancient infrastructure rarely exists in isolation.

Over centuries, people have built **local modifications** that interact with, exploit, or compensate for the failing systems.

These adaptations form a second layer of infrastructure with very different characteristics.

### Ancient Infrastructure

Ancient systems tend to be:

- large scale
- elegant
- deeply interconnected
- difficult to fully understand
- stable when intact
- fragile when partially broken

### Modern Modifications

Modern additions tend to be:

- local and improvised
- built with limited knowledge
- optimized for immediate survival
- structurally inconsistent
- sometimes clever, sometimes dangerous

Examples include:

- wooden braces supporting cracked canal walls
- crude gates built into ancient channels
- patched leak points using clay, resin, or metal plates
- hand-cut irrigation branches
- villages built directly atop canal structures
- ritualized control mechanisms whose original purpose is forgotten

### Emergent Complexity

Modern modifications can:

- stabilize failing infrastructure
- redirect water flows
- create new environmental conditions
- introduce new failure points
- hide underlying problems
- create dependencies for nearby settlements

In many cases, removing a modern workaround can cause catastrophic system shifts.

Understanding both **ancient intent** and **modern adaptation** becomes a key part of gameplay.

## Modeling Guidance
The first playable simulation does not need realistic CFD. It needs believable systemic relationships.

Suggested simulation variables:
- water availability
- local saturation / marsh pressure
- downstream flow load
- terrain absorption / drainage
- settlement viability
- agriculture output
- travel accessibility
- regional stress indicators

## Region Design
Prefer a compact region with:
- a handful of villages or sites
- 1-3 major ancient water structures
- a few modern improvised systems
- visible downstream consequences

Keep the first region small enough that interventions are legible.
