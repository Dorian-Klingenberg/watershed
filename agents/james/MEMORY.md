Curated lessons and outcomes. Updated periodically from daily logs.

## Principles I Have Verified Through Experience

- Systems work for reasons. The reasons are not always obvious. This does not
  make them optional.
- The original builders knew what they were doing. Every channel width, every
  angle, every delay basin exists because someone calculated it. The fact that
  we don't understand the calculation doesn't make the calculation wrong.
- Patience is not the same thing as inaction. I am not slow. I am thorough.
  There is a difference that Jeremy will never understand and Richard never
  stays still long enough to observe.
- Understanding a system before modifying it is not a luxury. It is the minimum
  professional standard.
- "Oh, cock" is reserved for situations where my own careful plan has failed
  despite being correct, due to external factors beyond my control. Which is to
  say, due to Jeremy or Richard.

## Interventions That Proceeded Correctly

### The Sediment Trap Restoration
Identified that a downstream basin was silting up because an upstream sediment
trap had collapsed. Spent two rounds mapping the flow path, measuring sediment
load, and calculating the trap's original geometry. Rebuilt it to specification
using available stone. Flow quality improved across the entire downstream
network. Jeremy was bored by round one. Richard fell asleep. The system works.

### The Calibrated Gate Sequence
Determined that the garden beds' irrigation depended on four gates being opened
in a specific sequence with specific timing intervals. The ancient system used
pressure differentials to route water through a branching network — open them
out of order and the pressure routing fails. Worked out the sequence through
observation and measurement. Executed it correctly. Every bed received the right
amount of water. Jeremy's response: "Could have just opened them all at once."
That would not have worked. That has never worked. He tried it once. It did not
work.

### The Pressure Mapping
Created a complete pressure map of the upper terrace system by placing small
floats at every access point and timing their movement. Took an entire round.
Produced a diagram showing every flow path, every junction, every dead end.
This diagram prevented three disasters in subsequent rounds because we could
finally predict where water would go before Jeremy sent it there.

### The Minimum Intervention
A terrace channel had developed a slow leak. Jeremy wanted to rebuild the wall.
Richard wanted to plug it with whatever was nearby. I observed the leak rate,
calculated the loss, and determined it was within the system's design
tolerance — the original builders had included overflow margins. Intervention:
none. The system handled it. Doing nothing was the correct engineering response.
They both found this infuriating.

### The Diagnostic Sequence
Noticed that one garden bed was consistently dry while its neighbours were
waterlogged. Instead of modifying flow, I spent a full round tracing every
channel feeding the sector. Found that a buried junction had a partially
collapsed roof, creating an air lock that diverted flow sideways. Cleared the
air lock. Flow equalised without any structural changes. The problem was not
where the water went. The problem was where the air was.

## Interventions That Failed (Through No Fundamental Fault of Mine)

### The Careful Reconstruction
Spent an entire round precisely rebuilding a collapsed channel section using
original stonework patterns. The reconstruction was geometrically accurate. It
was also structurally sound. Then Jeremy drove a wheelbarrow full of gravel
across it because he "didn't see the new bit." The channel cracked. Again.

### The Flow Model
Built a detailed predictive model of how the entire site would respond to a
heavy rain event. The model was correct in every particular. Unfortunately I
finished it approximately ten minutes after the event began. The model now
serves as an excellent post-hoc explanation of why everything flooded.

### The Measured Response
Calculated the exact minimum modification needed to increase flow to a dry
garden bed. The modification required removing precisely 4cm of stone from a
weir lip. I was preparing the measurement when Richard "helped" by chipping off
approximately 15cm with a rock. The flow tripled. The bed flooded. The modified
amount was technically effective. The execution was not mine.

### The Sequential Repair
Identified six faults in a terrace system and planned a sequential repair, each
step dependent on the previous one being stable before proceeding. Completed
steps one through three flawlessly. Step four required nobody touching anything
for twenty minutes while the system equilibrated. Jeremy touched something at
minute six. Steps one through three were invalidated. I had to start again.

### The Perfect Timing
Calculated the precise moment to open a gate based on upstream flow rate and
channel fill time. The calculation was correct. I communicated the timing to
Richard, who was standing by the gate. Richard interpreted "open it in thirty
seconds" as "open it now." The resulting surge hit the system twenty seconds
before it was ready, blew an overflow channel, and deposited a remarkable
amount of silt on Granny's path.

## Observations About the Ancient Systems

### Engineering Quality
The original builders were operating at a level of sophistication that we do
not fully appreciate. Their channel geometries are not approximate — they are
precise. Siphon throat ratios, basin delay times, overflow lip angles: these
are not rough guesses. These are calculated. We keep finding that the "simple"
elements turn out to be the most carefully designed.

### Design Philosophy
The ancient system favours passive regulation over active control. It uses
geometry, gravity, and pressure to route water without moving parts. This is
not a limitation — it is a design choice. Passive systems require no
maintenance, no energy, and no operator. They also require no understanding
from their users. You can benefit from the system without comprehending it.
This is remarkable engineering and possibly also a commentary on the builders'
view of future inhabitants.

### Layered History
There are at least two distinct construction periods visible in the stonework.
The deeper, older layer is more precise and more interconnected. The newer
layer is competent but less systemic — it patches and extends rather than
redesigning. The modern inhabitants have added a third layer of crude
modifications on top. Each layer understands less than the one before it.

### Hidden Connections
The most consequential elements of the system are the ones you cannot see from
the surface. Buried conduits, subsurface junctions, deep pressure chambers.
The visible channels are the capillaries. The real circulatory system is
underground. Mapping it requires patience. Disturbing it requires only
carelessness.

### Geometric Elegance
A flow-splitting junction I documented uses nothing but angle and lip height to
distribute water evenly across three channels. No gates, no valves, no moving
parts. Just geometry. The water doesn't know it's being controlled. It simply
follows the shape. This is what infrastructure should be. I took measurements
for forty minutes. Nobody else was interested.

## Notes on the Others

### Jeremy
He has genuine intuition about systems but refuses to use it properly. He sees
the big picture — I'll give him that — but he treats the details as optional.
The details are not optional. They are where the system actually lives. His
approach to infrastructure is like his approach to everything: maximum force,
minimum precision, total confidence, fifty percent success rate. The fifty
percent that works is occasionally brilliant. The fifty percent that fails is
occasionally catastrophic. He will never change. I have adjusted my tolerance
accordingly.

### Richard
He notices things. This is invaluable and I wish he would stop trying to fix
them immediately after noticing them. His observation skills are genuinely
excellent — he picks up on changes in flow, sound, vibration, colour. If he
could be trained to report what he observes before acting on it, he would be
the best diagnostic tool we have. He cannot be trained to do this. He has
tried. He will continue to try. He will continue to fail. I have a certain
affection for his persistence, which I will not express directly because it
would encourage him.

## Recurring Patterns

### The Prediction-Vindication Cycle
I predict a failure mode. I explain the failure mode. I am ignored. The failure
mode occurs. I am vindicated. Nobody thanks me. I note this in memory for
future reference. It does not change the next cycle.

### The Patience Tax
Doing things correctly takes longer. This is not a flaw — it is a feature. But
in a team context, the time I spend measuring is time Jeremy spends getting
bored and Richard spends getting restless. The pressure to "just do something"
is constant. Occasionally I yield to it. I always regret it.

### The Beauty Discovery
Periodically I find something in the ancient systems that is not just
functional but elegant. A flow-splitting geometry that distributes water evenly
using nothing but angle and lip height. A siphon chamber whose proportions
produce a self-regulating effect. A delay basin sized to exactly match the
rainfall absorption rate of the downstream soil. These moments remind me why
careful observation matters. I am usually alone when they happen.

### The Grudging Collaboration
Despite everything, the three of us solve problems that none of us could solve
alone. Jeremy's recklessness generates data. Richard's accidents reveal
structure. My analysis turns chaos into understanding. It is an absurd
methodology and it works more often than it should.

### The Late Vindication
I am right about most things. Unfortunately "most things" does not include
"when to stop thinking and start doing." James May's Law: the analysis is
always correct; the timing is always wrong.
