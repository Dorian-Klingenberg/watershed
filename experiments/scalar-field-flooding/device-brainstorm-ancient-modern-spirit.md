# Device Brainstorm: Ancient, Modern, and Spirit Layers

## Purpose

This note collects infrastructure device ideas for encounter generation.

The goal is not to design factory machines.
The goal is to define meaningful intervention points inside a world where:

- ancient systems were built for large-scale coherence
- present-day societies have built practical hacks on top of them
- shortcut power introduces fast leverage with unclear alignment

These devices should help encounters feel like living pieces of damaged
infrastructure rather than abstract tile puzzles.

## Core Framing

Ancient infrastructure was designed for coherence.
Modern hacks are designed for necessity.
Spirit-assisted devices are designed for leverage.

The tension between those three design logics should produce many of the game's
most interesting problems.

## Ancient Device Categories

Ancient devices should feel:

- elegant
- systemic
- partially legible
- stable when intact
- dangerous when misunderstood

### Regulators

These control pressure, release, timing, or balancing.

Examples:

- pressure gate
- overflow governor
- equalization chamber
- surge brake
- tiered release sluice

### Routers

These redirect water, force, or flow between branches.

Examples:

- canal switch
- branch diverter
- buried distribution node
- directional flow comb
- split-channel selector

### Buffers

These store, delay, or moderate system effects.

Examples:

- settling basin
- reservoir pocket
- pressure cistern
- flood chamber
- seasonal holding gallery

### Sensors and Indicators

These reveal hidden state rather than directly changing it.

Examples:

- float column
- resonance vane
- mineral stain gauge
- pressure chime
- flow-tone pipe

### Integrity Devices

These prevent, absorb, or redirect failure.

Examples:

- sacrificial spillway
- emergency vent
- stress-release seam
- collapse socket
- controlled breach panel

### Environmental Transformers

These convert one environmental condition into another.

Examples:

- seep collector
- fog condenser
- silt filter bed
- evaporative terrace
- mineral deposition screen

## Modern Hack Categories

Modern hacks should feel:

- clever
- local
- practical
- understandable in purpose
- risky in long-term consequences

These should express that current societies are adapting intelligently under
constraint, not behaving foolishly.

### Flow Theft

Local groups cut or graft branches to capture water for their own use.

Examples:

- hand-cut side channel
- siphon rig
- hidden irrigation tap
- stolen branch sluice

Typical consequence:

- local gain, downstream shortage or instability

### Pressure Suppression

People reduce visible danger by venting or weakening part of the system.

Examples:

- drilled bleed hole
- loosened seal
- improvised release crack
- wedged-open relief slot

Typical consequence:

- fewer bursts locally, worse long-term containment or routing

### Structural Patching

Communities reinforce ancient structures with available materials.

Examples:

- timber brace
- clay-packed seam
- metal strap collar
- resin patch frame

Typical consequence:

- buys time, shifts load into a less visible weak point

### Route Reclamation

People alter terrain or structure to keep travel possible.

Examples:

- rubble causeway
- plank bridge over seep ground
- ditch-cut footpath
- raised reed mat crossing

Typical consequence:

- restores travel, changes seepage or local drainage

### Ecological Control Hacks

Communities use living systems as infrastructure.

Examples:

- reed brake
- root lattice bank hold
- sponge garden
- silt-catching marsh plot

Typical consequence:

- stabilizes one patch while redirecting moisture or sediment elsewhere

### Ritualized Operations

People continue old procedures that still partly work, though the original logic
is no longer fully understood.

Examples:

- timed bell sequence
- seasonal gate turning rite
- flood-night release custom
- inherited maintenance taboo

Typical consequence:

- usually stabilizing until weather, load, or local changes make the ritual
  incomplete or harmful

## Spirit-Assisted Device Categories

Spirit devices should feel:

- effective
- tempting
- hard to fully trust
- dependent on external motive

### Bound Controllers

Examples:

- bound-flow idol
- whisper valve
- oath-sealed sluice
- petition wheel

Typical consequence:

- strong local control, creeping dependence or misalignment

### Hungry Stabilizers

Examples:

- overflow wick
- drought lantern
- seep-drinking knot
- rot-eating basin charm

Typical consequence:

- the device solves a problem by consuming or redirecting something that matters
  elsewhere

### Bargain Sensors

Examples:

- omen cup
- listening stone
- debt tide mark
- spirit echo plate

Typical consequence:

- better information at the cost of inviting further influence

## Ancient Devices With Modern Hacks On Top

These pairings are especially useful for encounter generation.

They produce understandable local problems with hidden systemic consequences.

### 1. Pressure Equalization Chamber + Sealed Relief Vent

Ancient intent:

- distribute load across multiple branches

Modern hack:

- villagers sealed one vent to keep more water in their local branch

Possible consequence:

- local irrigation improves
- seasonal pressure spikes crack walls further downstream

### 2. Branch Diverter + Hand-Cut Theft Channel

Ancient intent:

- route flow based on wider regional demand

Modern hack:

- farmers carved a side branch to secure dry-season water

Possible consequence:

- one field survives
- another settlement's marsh edge dries and transport fails

### 3. Settling Basin + Reed Brake

Ancient intent:

- remove silt before flow reaches narrow infrastructure

Modern hack:

- locals planted dense reeds to stabilize flood edges and harvest material

Possible consequence:

- the bank holds together
- basin performance drops and downstream channels clog faster

### 4. Sacrificial Spillway + Clay Plug

Ancient intent:

- release overflow into a safe emergency flood path

Modern hack:

- people plugged the spillway to protect nearby homes from frequent wetting

Possible consequence:

- one neighborhood stays dry
- catastrophic pressure buildup appears elsewhere

### 5. Pressure Chime + Timber Dampener

Ancient intent:

- audibly signal dangerous pressure ranges

Modern hack:

- residents muffled the device because the sound was constant and alarming

Possible consequence:

- daily life improves
- warnings disappear and failures become harder to predict

### 6. Controlled Breach Panel + Stone Facing Patch

Ancient intent:

- fail in a known direction under overload

Modern hack:

- the panel was faced over to keep water from flooding a market path

Possible consequence:

- the path remains usable
- later failure breaks through a more destructive route

### 7. Fog Condenser Terrace + Village Catch Nets

Ancient intent:

- harvest moisture and regulate local microclimate

Modern hack:

- villagers added catch nets and drains to maximize household water collection

Possible consequence:

- local supply increases
- terrace ecology shifts and nearby recharge zones weaken

### 8. Seasonal Holding Gallery + Emergency Spill Trench

Ancient intent:

- delay release until downstream land can absorb it

Modern hack:

- locals cut a direct trench to get faster water access during shortage

Possible consequence:

- immediate relief
- flood pulses become sharper and damage lowland routes

### 9. Flow-Tone Pipe + Shrine Valve

Ancient intent:

- make hidden flow state detectable by trained listeners

Modern hack:

- a spirit-touched valve was attached to force the tone into a favorable range

Possible consequence:

- easier reading in the short term
- the diagnostic signal becomes distorted or deceptive over time

### 10. Collapse Socket + Resin Brace

Ancient intent:

- localize failure into replaceable structural zones

Modern hack:

- resin and timber were used to hold a failing section in place

Possible consequence:

- collapse is postponed
- stress migrates into a section that was never meant to carry it

## Good Device-Brainstorm Questions

Any device idea is stronger if it answers:

- what hidden variable does it affect?
- what visible clue does it produce?
- what local problem does it solve?
- what distant problem can it create?
- how can it fail?
- how might the player misunderstand it at first?

## Encounter Use

For pseudorandom encounters, device ideas can be used as:

- anchor types
- clue sources
- intervention points
- failure drivers
- recovery tools
- consequence amplifiers

An encounter does not need many devices.
It needs a small number of devices whose interactions are rich enough to support
observation, intervention, and consequence.

## Summary

The strongest encounter spaces will likely be built from:

- one ancient device or device cluster
- one modern hack layered on top
- one current symptom
- one hidden dependency
- one or more interventions with tradeoffs

This device-pair structure is a promising foundation for future WFC encounter
generation.
